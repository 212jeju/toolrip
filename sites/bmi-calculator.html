    BMI Categories

    The World Health Organization (WHO) defines the following BMI categories for adults. These ranges apply to both men and women aged 20 and older. Note that BMI may be interpreted differently for children, teens, and older adults.

    Category BMI Range (kg/m²) Health Risk
    Severe Thinness < 16.0 High
    Moderate Thinness 16.0 – 16.9 Moderate
    Mild Thinness 17.0 – 18.4 Low
    Normal Weight 18.5 – 24.9 Low
    Overweight 25.0 – 29.9 Increased
    Obese Class I 30.0 – 34.9 High
    Obese Class II 35.0 – 39.9 Very High
    Obese Class III ≥ 40.0 Extremely High

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is BMI?

    Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from a person's weight and height. It was developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the early 19th century and is widely used as a simple screening tool to categorize individuals into weight categories: underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obese. While BMI does not directly measure body fat, it correlates with more direct measures of body fat such as skinfold thickness, bioelectrical impedance, and dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry. Healthcare providers use BMI as a first-line assessment to identify potential weight-related health risks in populations and individuals alike.

    How is BMI calculated?

    BMI is calculated by dividing a person's weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters. The formula is: BMI = weight (kg) / height (m)². For imperial units, the formula is: BMI = (weight in pounds / height in inches²) × 703. For example, a person weighing 70 kg with a height of 1.75 meters would have a BMI of 70 / (1.75 × 1.75) = 22.86, which falls within the normal weight range. Our calculator handles both metric and imperial conversions automatically so you do not need to perform manual calculations.

    What is a healthy BMI range?

    According to the World Health Organization, a healthy BMI range for adults is between 18.5 and 24.9 kg/m². This range is associated with the lowest risk of weight-related health problems. A BMI below 18.5 indicates underweight, which can be associated with nutritional deficiencies, weakened immune function, and bone density loss. A BMI between 25 and 29.9 is classified as overweight, and a BMI of 30 or above is classified as obese. However, the healthy range may vary slightly depending on factors such as ethnicity, age, and individual body composition. For Asian populations, for example, some health authorities recommend lower thresholds due to higher health risks at lower BMI values.

    Is BMI accurate for athletes?

    BMI has notable limitations when applied to athletes and highly muscular individuals. Because BMI uses total body weight rather than distinguishing between muscle mass and fat mass, athletes with significant muscle development may be classified as overweight or even obese despite having low body fat percentages. For example, a bodybuilder or rugby player may have a BMI of 30 or higher while carrying minimal excess fat. In these cases, alternative measurements such as body fat percentage, waist-to-hip ratio, or DEXA scans provide more accurate assessments of body composition and health risk. If you are an athlete or have above-average muscle mass, consider using BMI alongside other metrics for a more complete picture.

    Does age affect BMI interpretation?

    Yes, age can influence how BMI should be interpreted. For children and teenagers aged 2 to 19, BMI is calculated the same way but is interpreted using age- and sex-specific percentile charts because body composition changes significantly during growth and development. For older adults over 65, research suggests that a slightly higher BMI range of 23 to 30 may be associated with lower mortality rates. This is sometimes referred to as the "obesity paradox." Older adults with very low BMI may face increased risks from malnutrition, muscle wasting (sarcopenia), and frailty. Therefore, while BMI remains a useful screening tool across all ages, the interpretation of results should account for the individual's life stage.

    What are the limitations of BMI?

    While BMI is a convenient and widely used screening tool, it has several important limitations. First, it does not differentiate between lean muscle mass, bone density, and fat tissue, meaning muscular individuals may be misclassified. Second, it does not account for fat distribution. Visceral fat (abdominal fat) poses significantly greater health risks than subcutaneous fat, but BMI cannot distinguish between the two. Third, BMI does not consider differences in body composition related to sex, ethnicity, or age. Fourth, it provides no information about metabolic health markers such as blood pressure, cholesterol, or blood sugar levels. For these reasons, medical professionals typically use BMI in conjunction with other assessments including waist circumference measurement, blood tests, physical examinations, and lifestyle evaluations to form a comprehensive health assessment.
