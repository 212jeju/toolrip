How to Use the Salary to Hourly Calculator

  1. Select your conversion mode using the tabs at the top: choose "Annual Salary to Hourly" if you know your yearly pay, or "Hourly to Annual Salary" if you know your hourly wage. Then enter the corresponding amount in the input field.
  2. Adjust the hours per week (default 40), weeks per year (default 52), and holidays or vacation days (default 10) to match your specific work schedule. These parameters ensure the calculation reflects your real-world situation.
  3. Review the full pay breakdown panel showing your hourly, daily, weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, monthly, quarterly, and annual figures. Check the adjusted section for your effective hourly rate after accounting for paid time off, and compare scenarios in the weekly hours comparison table.
Pro Tip

When comparing job offers, convert everything to an effective hourly rate. A $100K salary with 60-hour weeks ($32/hr) may be worse than $80K with 40-hour weeks ($38/hr). Include benefits value: employer health insurance is worth $7,000-$20,000 annually.

Common Mistake

Comparing gross salaries without factoring in state income tax differences. A $90,000 salary in Texas (no state tax) gives you more take-home pay than a $95,000 salary in California (up to 13.3% state tax). Always compare net pay, not gross, when evaluating offers across state lines.

Expert Insight

A $75,000 salary with employer-sponsored health insurance ($12,000 value), 5% 401(k) match ($3,750), and 20 PTO days ($5,769 value) has a true total compensation of roughly $96,500. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I compare two job offers with different pay structures?

Convert everything to an effective hourly rate for an apples-to-apples comparison. Enter each offer's base salary or hourly rate and adjust the hours-per-week field to reflect the actual expected workload. A position offering $100,000 but expecting 60-hour weeks works out to $32/hour effective, while $80,000 at a standard 40-hour week is $38/hour. Then factor in PTO differences using the holiday and vacation inputs — an offer with 25 PTO days at $70,000 has a higher effective hourly rate than $73,000 with only 10 PTO days. Beyond what this calculator shows, estimate benefits value: employer health insurance ($7,000-$20,000/year), 401(k) matching (3-6% of salary), commuting costs, and remote work flexibility. Adding these to the base salary before calculating the hourly rate gives you the most accurate comparison possible.

How much does working overtime actually increase my annual income?

For non-exempt (hourly) employees, overtime pays 1.5 times your regular rate for any hours beyond 40 per week. If you earn $25/hour and consistently work 50-hour weeks, you receive $1,000 in regular pay plus $375 in overtime (10 hours at $37.50), totaling $1,375 per week or approximately $71,500 annually. That is a 37.5% increase over the $52,000 base salary at 40 hours. However, overtime income is taxed at your marginal rate, not your average rate, so the take-home increase is smaller than it appears. Salaried exempt employees receive no additional compensation for hours beyond 40, which is why converting salary to an effective hourly rate (accounting for actual hours worked) is essential for understanding your true earning power. Use the weekly hours comparison table in this calculator to see how different hour levels affect your pay.

Should I negotiate salary or hourly rate when accepting a new position?

Consider the total compensation package, not just the headline number. Salaried positions typically include benefits worth an additional 20-40% on top of base pay, including health insurance, retirement plan matching, paid time off, and professional development budgets. Hourly positions offer overtime eligibility and clearer work-life boundaries. Before negotiating, request the full compensation breakdown from the employer, including all benefits and their estimated dollar values. If an employer offers $70,000 salary with comprehensive benefits (worth roughly $20,000), the total package is $90,000. An hourly role paying $40/hour at 40 hours/week totals $83,200 gross without benefits, so you would need to purchase your own insurance and retirement savings. Use this calculator to model different scenarios and find the minimum rate or salary that meets your financial needs.

How do holidays and vacation days change my real hourly earnings?

Paid time off reduces your working hours while keeping your salary the same, which raises your effective hourly rate. Consider a $60,000 salary with a 40-hour week: the base rate is $28.85/hour over 2,080 annual hours. With 10 holidays and 15 vacation days (25 total PTO days), you actually work 1,880 hours, making your effective rate $31.91/hour — a $3.06 increase per hour worked. This matters when comparing job offers: an employer offering $60,000 with 25 PTO days values your time more per hour than an employer offering $62,000 with only 10 PTO days ($62,000 / 1,960 hours = $31.63/hour). Enter different PTO scenarios into this calculator to see the impact on your effective rate instantly.

How do I estimate my take-home pay after taxes and deductions?

Start with your gross salary and subtract mandatory deductions: federal income tax (10-37% in progressive brackets), state income tax (0% in nine states like Texas and Florida, up to 13.3% in California), Social Security (6.2% up to the wage base limit), and Medicare (1.45% with no cap). On a $75,000 salary in a state with 5% income tax, expect approximately 73% take-home pay, or about $4,567 per month. Pre-tax deductions like 401(k) contributions and health insurance premiums reduce your taxable income, lowering your effective tax rate. The educational section below this FAQ provides a detailed breakdown of each deduction type with example calculations at different salary levels.

Understanding Your Take-Home Pay

Your gross salary and your take-home pay are two very different numbers. Between your employer issuing your paycheck and the money hitting your bank account, several mandatory deductions reduce the total. Understanding each deduction helps you plan your budget accurately and avoid surprises when you receive your first paycheck at a new job.

Gross Salary
Your total compensation before any deductions. This is the number you negotiate during the hiring process. For example, if you earn $75,000 per year, your gross monthly pay is $6,250.
Federal Income Tax
The United States uses a progressive tax system with seven brackets ranging from 10% to 37%. Only the income within each bracket is taxed at that rate. On a $75,000 salary filing single in 2024, your effective federal tax rate is approximately 14.3%, or roughly $893 per month.
State Income Tax
Varies dramatically by state. Nine states charge no income tax at all, including Texas, Florida, and Washington. Others, like California, can charge up to 13.3% at the highest bracket. For a $75,000 salary in a state with a 5% flat rate, this deduction is about $312 per month.
Social Security (FICA)
A flat 6.2% of your gross pay up to the wage base limit of $168,600 in 2024. On a $75,000 salary, this equals $4,650 per year or $387.50 per month. Your employer matches this amount.
Medicare
A flat 1.45% of all gross earnings with no cap. For $75,000, this is $1,087.50 per year or about $90.63 per month. Earners above $200,000 pay an additional 0.9% surtax.
Net (Take-Home) Pay
What remains after all deductions. On a $75,000 salary in a state with 5% income tax, your approximate monthly take-home pay is around $4,567 — about 73% of your gross. Optional deductions like 401(k) contributions, health insurance premiums, and HSA contributions will reduce this further.

Keep in mind that pre-tax deductions like 401(k) contributions and health insurance premiums reduce your taxable income, which can lower your effective tax rate. Contributing enough to your employer's 401(k) to get the full company match is often considered one of the best financial decisions you can make, since it is essentially free money added to your retirement savings.
