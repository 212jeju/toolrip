About David

David started his career in 2011 as a personal banker at a large regional bank in New Jersey. His first week, he watched a couple in their early sixties sit down and realize, together, that they were going to have to keep working for another decade — not because they'd done anything egregiously wrong, but because nobody had ever sat them down and shown them what compound interest actually looks like on a 30-year curve. He thinks about that conversation a lot. It's probably why he's still doing this.

After three years on the retail banking side, he moved into wealth advisory at a mid-sized independent firm in the Philadelphia suburbs. He got his CFP designation in 2016 — the exam rate was brutal that year, and he won't pretend he passed on the first try — and spent the next nine years as a fee-only planner working primarily with middle-income households. Not the ultra-wealthy clients that most financial media seems to write for. Real people: dual-income couples making $150K trying to figure out if they could afford a house, single parents juggling 529 contributions and credit card debt, recently widowed clients who had never written a check in their lives.

He left active advisory practice in early 2024 to focus on writing and financial education full-time. The short version of why: he was tired of having the same conversation 40 times a month when the information should have been freely available, clearly explained, and calculator-backed from the start. The longer version involves a lot of opinions about the financial media industry that he mostly keeps to himself.

David's writing style is what it is because of those twelve years across the desk from real clients. He doesn't write in abstractions. Every piece he does for Toolrip starts from a specific version of a question he actually got asked — usually multiple times — and works backward to the math. When he talks about mortgage amortization, he's thinking about the young teacher who came in convinced she couldn't afford a house because her parents told her 20% down was mandatory. (It isn't. She's a homeowner now.) When he writes about inflation, he's thinking about the retiree who'd been holding 90% cash since 2009 because one bad experience in 2008 had scared him permanently. Anecdotes are anonymized and sometimes composited, but the patterns are real.

He is no longer a practicing CFP — he let the licensure lapse when he stopped taking clients, both for compliance simplicity and because he did not want any of his writing to be confused with personalized financial advice. It isn't. Toolrip content is educational. For your situation specifically, see a professional who knows your full picture.

David lives outside Philadelphia with his wife, two kids, and a spreadsheet habit that his family finds either endearing or alarming depending on the day.
