About Elena

Elena came to design sideways, which she thinks is the best way to come to it. She studied Visual Communication at the Rhode Island School of Design, graduated in 2013, and spent her first two post-grad years doing print layout and brand identity work at a small studio in Providence. It was good, slow work — the kind where you obsess over kerning — but she found herself increasingly pulled toward screen-based projects and the strange, messier problems they presented.

In 2015 she moved to Brooklyn and took a junior UX role at a mid-sized e-commerce agency whose clients were mostly direct-to-consumer brands in fashion and home goods. She spent four years there, eventually leading design on a handful of replatforming projects. Somewhere in year two, she was auditing a client's checkout flow and realized the primary CTA — a pale pink button on a light gray background — was effectively invisible to a meaningful slice of users. Including, it turned out, the client's own father, who had age-related macular degeneration and had quietly stopped using the site. That moment rewired her whole practice.

She took the IAAP's Certified Professional in Accessibility Core Competencies (CPACC) exam in 2019 and passed it on her second attempt (the first attempt, she'll admit, was a humbling experience). Since then, accessibility has been the lens she applies to everything — color systems, component libraries, form design, motion preferences, the whole stack. She joined a larger product design team at a Seattle-based e-commerce platform in 2020 and spent four years there building out their design system with WCAG 2.1 AA conformance as a baseline requirement, not an afterthought.

Elena went independent in late 2024, splitting her time between consulting with small product teams on accessibility remediation and writing for publications like Toolrip. She took on Toolrip's design and color content specifically because color accessibility is wildly misunderstood — people think it's about following a rule, when really it's about understanding how human vision, context, and emotion interact with the pigments on a screen. She wants more designers and developers to feel fluent in that conversation, not just compliant with it.

Her writing leans human-centered on purpose. She'll talk about contrast ratios and APCA and WCAG thresholds, yes — but she'll also tell you about the user research session where a participant with low vision said "I just gave up on that page" and everything it taught her about the cost of invisible exclusion. Accessibility is a design discipline, not a checklist. She treats it that way on the page too.

Elena lives in Seattle with her partner and a very loud tortoiseshell cat named Pantone. She collects vintage design books, grows more houseplants than her apartment can really support, and maintains a personal swatch library that her friends find funny and her clients find useful.
