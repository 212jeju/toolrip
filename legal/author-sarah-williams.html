About Sarah

Sarah ran the 5K and 10K for Villanova from 2009 through 2013, which sounds more glamorous than it was. Mostly it was 80-mile weeks, shin splints, and a lot of oatmeal. She graduated with a B.S. in Exercise Science and a slightly unhealthy relationship with her Garmin, then spent a year coaching high school cross country in suburban Pennsylvania while she figured out what to do next.

What she did next was get her NASM-CPT in 2015 and move to Denver, where she worked as a trainer at a mid-sized commercial gym for three years. That's where she learned most of what she actually knows about coaching real people — not the college-athlete version of people, but the "I sit at a desk for nine hours and my left knee clicks" version. She worked with hundreds of clients: new moms getting back into movement, runners training for their first half marathon, older adults rebuilding strength after hip surgery, and a surprising number of software engineers who just wanted to not feel like garbage by Friday afternoon.

In 2018 she added her Precision Nutrition Level 1 certification because roughly 70% of the questions her clients asked her were about food, and she was tired of saying "I'm not a dietitian, but..." She still isn't a dietitian, and she'll tell you so — but she's comfortable with the evidence base on calorie balance, macronutrients, and behavior change, and she's honest about where coaching ends and clinical nutrition begins.

These days Sarah coaches runners remotely through her own small practice and writes part-time for Toolrip. She joined in early 2025 because, frankly, she was fed up with the state of health content online. Most of what she read was either pseudo-scientific wellness nonsense or overly academic journal-summary content that nobody could actually use. She wanted to write the thing in the middle — real science, real numbers, but framed for someone who just wants to know if their resting heart rate of 72 is okay. (It's fine. It's fine.)

She's a firm believer that most "fitness math" tools — BMI, TDEE, one-rep max estimators, pace calculators — are useful precisely because they give you a starting point, and dangerous precisely because people treat them as final verdicts. Every article she writes tries to hold both of those things at once. Numbers are information. Information isn't identity.

Sarah still runs, though slower now and with more rest days than her 21-year-old self would have tolerated. Her current PR is a 1:24 half marathon from 2022. She lives in Boulder with her husband, a border collie named Sprint (yes, really), and an increasingly elaborate collection of foam rollers.
